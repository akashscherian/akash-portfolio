GRC project · 2024

ISO 27001:2022 risk assessment — pharma e-log system

A full ISMS-style risk assessment for an electronic-log system used in pharmaceutical manufacturing, mapping security risks to both ISO 27001:2022 Annex A controls and 21 CFR Part 11 requirements.

ISO 27001:2022 21 CFR Part 11 GxP / ALCOA+ Risk register Threat modelling

Context

Pharma manufacturing sites are required to keep detailed logs of every action that touches a regulated product — cleaning logs, calibration logs, deviation logs. Historically these were paper; the move to e-logs introduces a new attack surface and a new compliance burden. The system has to satisfy two overlapping frameworks at once: ISO 27001 (information security) and 21 CFR Part 11 (FDA rules on electronic records and electronic signatures).

Approach

I followed the ISO 27001:2022 Clause 6.1.2 risk-assessment process, adapted for the pharma context:

  1. Asset inventory — every information asset that the e-log system touches: user accounts, audit-trail records, batch data, the application server, the database, backups, network paths.
  2. Threat & vulnerability identification — for each asset, what could go wrong (insider modifies a log entry, audit trail tampered with, credentials shared, backups lost).
  3. Risk analysis — likelihood × impact scoring on a 1–5 scale, producing a risk score and risk level (Low / Medium / High / Critical).
  4. Risk treatment plan — for each non-acceptable risk: a treatment option (mitigate / transfer / avoid / accept) plus the specific Annex A controls and 21 CFR Part 11 sections that justify the mitigation.

Example: audit-trail tampering risk

This is the canonical pharma risk — and a useful one to walk through because it sits at the intersection of both frameworks.

Risk: An operator with system access modifies an entry in the e-log after the fact to hide a deviation.
Likelihood: 2 (low — requires intent + access) · Impact: 5 (regulatory fine, product recall) · Score: 10 (High)

Treatment: mitigate via three layered controls:

  • ISO 27001 A.8.15 — Logging: immutable, append-only audit trail; every change captured as a new event, never an overwrite.
  • ISO 27001 A.5.18 — Access rights: separation of duties so the person creating a log cannot approve/close it.
  • 21 CFR Part 11 §11.10(e): the audit trail must be computer-generated, time-stamped, and capture the operator, the original value, and the new value.

Deliverables

  • Risk register — spreadsheet listing every identified risk with score, owner, treatment plan, target date, and current status.
  • Control gap analysis — for each Annex A control, whether it was implemented, partially implemented, or missing, with evidence pointers.
  • Threat model summary — one-page diagram of the e-log system's data flows with trust boundaries marked, and the top threats per boundary.
  • Risk treatment plan — prioritised list of remediations, ordered by risk score and effort.

What I learned

  • The two frameworks aren't redundant — they reinforce each other. ISO 27001 tells you how to protect information; 21 CFR Part 11 tells you what evidence regulators need to see. A good control satisfies both.
  • Risk scoring is the easy part. Owner assignment is the hard part. Every risk needs a name next to it, and getting people to accept ownership is the actual work.
  • Audit-readiness ≠ security. A system can be fully documented and still insecure. The risk register surfaces gaps the docs hide.
Why this matters for SOC/security roles: compliance frameworks aren't bureaucracy — they're the language a security team uses to justify spend, prioritise alerts, and explain risk to leadership. Knowing how to map a technical control back to ISO/PCI/CFR makes you immediately useful.
